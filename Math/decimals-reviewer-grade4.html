🔟 Part 1: Tenths

When we cut something into 10 equal parts, each part is called a tenth.

Imagine a chocolate bar cut into 10 equal pieces.
If you eat 3 pieces, you ate: 310 = 0.3 We say this as: "zero point three"

🟠 = shaded (tenths counted)   ⬜ = empty

💡 The digit after the decimal point (.) is the tenths place!
0.3 — the 3 is in the tenths place = 3 tenths

📝 How to Write Tenths

  1. Look at the fraction: the bottom number (denominator) is 10.
  2. The top number (numerator) becomes the digit after the decimal point.
  3. Always write 0. first, then the numerator.
710 = 0.7  (7 tenths)

110 = 0.1  (1 tenth)
💡 Going the other way: 0.6 = 6/10 (put the digit over 10!)

🔀 Mixed Numbers with Tenths

A mixed number has a whole number AND a fraction.

2 510 = 2 + 0.5 = 2.5

3 410 = 3 + 0.4 = 3.4
  1. Keep the whole number on the left of the decimal point.
  2. Convert the fraction part to a decimal.
  3. Put them together!

💯 Part 2: Hundredths

When something is cut into 100 equal parts, each part is called a hundredth.

37100 = 0.37

We say: "zero point three seven"
Ones.TenthsHundredths
0.37
💡 The 1st digit after the point = tenths
The 2nd digit after the point = hundredths

🔄 Fractions to Hundredths Decimals

If the denominator is NOT 100, change it to 100 first!

  1. Find what number multiplies the bottom to get 100.
  2. Multiply the top by the same number.
  3. Now write the decimal!
Example: 34
4 × 25 = 100, so 3 × 25 = 75
34 = 75100 = 0.75
Example: 534
34 = 0.75  →  5.75

🔢 Part 3: Thousandths

Cut something into 1,000 equal parts — each part is a thousandth!

71000 = 0.007
We say: "seven thousandths"

0.007
4.039
💡 Count the digits after the decimal point:
1 digit = tenths  |  2 digits = hundredths  |  3 digits = thousandths

🧮 Examples with Thousandths

  1. 251000 = 0.025  → "25 thousandths"
  2. 1721000 = 0.172  → "172 thousandths"
  3. 4391000 = 4 + 0.039 = 4.039
💡 For 391000: we need 3 digits after the point → 0.039 (add a zero!)

📏 Part 4: Reading Number Lines

A number line shows decimals in order. Between any two whole numbers, there are 10 equal spaces.

🔴 Point A = 0.2  (2 steps from 0)
🟣 Point B = 0.8  (8 steps from 0)
🟢 Point C = 1.7  (7 steps past 1)
💡 Each small step = 0.1  →  Count the steps from the nearest whole number!

🧭 How to Read a Number Line

  1. Find the two whole numbers on either side of the point.
  2. Count how many equal spaces are between them (usually 10).
  3. Count how many steps the point is from the left whole number.
  4. Each step = 0.1  →  Add that to the whole number.
A point is between 1 and 2, and it's 4 steps from 1:
1 + 4 × 0.1 = 1 + 0.4 = 1.4

✂️ Part 5: Simplest Form

We can write decimals as fractions or mixed numbers in their simplest form by dividing the top and bottom by the same number.

Example: Convert 0.5 to simplest form
0.5 = 510  ÷ 5 both →   12   ✅
Example: Convert 2.6 to simplest form
2.6 = 2 + 0.6 = 2 + 610 = 2 + 35 = 235 ✅

🪜 Steps to Simplify

  1. Write the decimal as a fraction (put digits over 10, 100, or 1000).
  2. Find the GCF (biggest number that divides both top and bottom).
  3. Divide top AND bottom by the GCF.
  4. If there's a whole number part, put it back!
0.028:
= 281000  ÷ 4 both →   7250 ✅

🔁 Mixed Numbers as Decimals

To go from a mixed number to a decimal: change the fraction part's denominator to 10, 100, or 1000 first!

  1. Look at the fraction part (like 34).
  2. Multiply to get denominator 100: 4 × 25 = 100, so 3 × 25 = 75.
  3. 34 = 75100 = 0.75
  4. Add the whole number: 5 + 0.75 = 5.75

⚖️ Part 6: Comparing Decimals

To compare decimals, we look at the digits one by one from left to right.

NumberOnes.TenthsHundredths
4.524.52
5.125.12
4.584.58
💡 Step 1: Compare ones — 5 > 4, so 5.12 is greatest!
Step 2: Between 4.52 and 4.58 — same ones and tenths, compare hundredths: 8 > 2, so 4.58 > 4.52

📋 Steps to Compare

  1. Line up the decimal points.
  2. Compare ones (left of decimal) first.
  3. If ones are equal, compare tenths.
  4. If tenths are equal, compare hundredths.
  5. Keep going until you find a difference!
Order from smallest to greatest: 3.7, 3.07, 3.70
Ones all = 3 → compare tenths: 0 < 7 = 7
Smallest to greatest: 3.07, 3.7, 3.70
(Note: 3.7 = 3.70 — they're equal!)

🔀 Comparing Decimals & Fractions

If you need to compare a decimal and a fraction, convert the fraction to a decimal first!

Compare 0.42, 14, and 0.412

14 = 25100 = 0.25
Now compare: 0.42, 0.25, 0.412
Smallest to greatest: 0.25, 0.412, 0.42
