📖 ENGLISH READING
📝 Topic: Reading Comprehension — Finding the Main Idea

When we read a passage, we need to understand what it is mostly about. That "mostly about" part is called the main idea.

💡 Main Idea = The most important point the author wants you to know. It is not just one detail — it covers the whole passage.

Supporting details are the facts, examples, or reasons that prove or explain the main idea.

🔍 How to Find the Main Idea
  1. Read the whole passage — don't stop at one sentence.
  2. Ask yourself: "What is this mostly about?"
  3. Check the first and last sentences — the main idea is often there.
  4. Test your idea: Do the other sentences support it?
🚫 Trap! A detail is not the main idea. If a sentence only talks about one small part, it's a supporting detail.
📄 Sample Passage
Read this:

Dogs are one of the most popular pets in the world. They are loyal and enjoy spending time with their owners. Dogs can be trained to do many helpful things, such as guiding blind people, detecting danger, and even helping police officers. Because of their intelligence and friendly nature, dogs are often called "man's best friend."

Main Idea: Dogs make great pets and helpers because they are loyal and intelligent.

Supporting Details:

  • They guide blind people.
  • They can detect danger.
  • They help police officers.
📚 Vocabulary Builder

Strong readers know how to figure out word meanings from context. Look at the words around an unfamiliar word — they give you clues!

🌟 Types of Text

Knowing the type of text helps you understand it better.

  • Narrative — tells a story with characters and events.
  • Informational / Expository — explains facts about a topic (like the dog passage above).
  • Persuasive — tries to convince you to agree with an opinion.
  • Descriptive — paints a picture with words.
🎯 The dog passage is informational — it gives facts. No story, no opinions. Just facts and explanations.

🔢 MATHEMATICS
➕ Topic: Fractions & Decimals

A fraction shows a part of a whole. A decimal is another way to write a fraction — using a decimal point instead.

💡 Remember: The numerator is on top (the part you have). The denominator is on the bottom (the total parts).
🔄 Converting Fractions to Decimals

To convert a fraction to a decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator.

Example 1: ½ → 1 ÷ 2 = 0.5
Example 2: ¾ → 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75
Example 3: ⅖ → 2 ÷ 5 = 0.4
📌 Common ones to memorize:
½ = 0.5  |  ¼ = 0.25  |  ¾ = 0.75  |  ⅕ = 0.2  |  ⅖ = 0.4
➕ Adding & Subtracting Fractions

Same denominator → Just add/subtract the numerators!

Example: 2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7   ✅ Easy!

Different denominators → Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD) first.

Example: 1/3 + 1/4
→ LCD of 3 and 4 = 12
→ 1/3 = 4/12    1/4 = 3/12
→ 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12 ✅
🔑 Finding LCD: List the multiples of each denominator. The smallest number on both lists is the LCD.
✖️ Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is actually easier — multiply straight across!

Example 1: 2/3 × 3/5
→ Numerators: 2 × 3 = 6
→ Denominators: 3 × 5 = 15
→ Answer: 6/15 → Simplify → 2/5 ✅
Example 2: 1/2 × 4/7
→ 1 × 4 = 4  |  2 × 7 = 14
→ Answer: 4/14 → Simplify → 2/7 ✅
🧹 Simplify! Always reduce your answer to its simplest form. Divide numerator and denominator by their GCF (Greatest Common Factor).
📊 Comparing Fractions & Decimals

To compare fractions easily, convert them to decimals first.

Which is bigger: 3/5 or 5/8?
3/5 = 0.60
5/8 = 0.625
→ 0.625 > 0.60, so 5/8 is bigger ✅

You can also use cross-multiplication:

Compare 2/3 and 3/4:
→ 2 × 4 = 8   |   3 × 3 = 9
→ 9 > 8, so 3/4 > 2/3 ✅
