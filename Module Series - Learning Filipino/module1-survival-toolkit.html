THE RESPECT CODE
Po and Opo
In Filipino culture, respect — paggalang — is non-negotiable. Without it, conversations feel cold, like a locked door. Po and opo are your keys. 🗝️
THE PASSCODE RULE
Think of po and opo like a Face ID. Without them, the elder can't "unlock" you as someone respectful. You can't enter the inner circle of trust!
📱🔓
What's the difference?
📝
PO
Lives inside a sentence. It attaches to words — usually after the 1st or 2nd word. It's like a politeness sticker you add to sentences.
Salamat po

Hindi ko po alam
✅
OPO
A standalone polite "YES." Used only when answering yes to an elder. It replaces the casual "oo" (yes).
Opo!

Opo, po.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE
Don't mix up po and opo!
Wrong: "Opo, opo, Teacher!" — Using opo inside a sentence sounds childish.

Right: "Opo, Teacher!" then "Paki-ulit po." — Opo stands alone; po joins sentences.

THE KINSHIP INTERFACE
Ate and Kuya
In Filipino culture, you don't just call strangers "Sir" or "Ma'am" — you bring them closer by treating them like family! Ate and Kuya turn a stranger into a sibling instantly.
📷 THE CAMERA ZOOM RULE
Sir / Ma'am = Wide-angle lens 📷 → keeps distance, stays professional

Ate / Kuya = Zoom lens 🔍 → brings people closer, places them in your "family photo"
👧
ATE (Ah-teh)
Used for older girls/women — classmates, school staff, strangers who seem older than you. Not just real sisters!
Ate, pakiabot po ng tubig.
👦
KUYA (Ku-ya)
Used for older boys/men — older classmates, tricycle drivers, older schoolmates. Creates warmth instantly!
Kuya, magkano po ito?

SOFTENING COMMANDS
The Paki- Magic Prefix
In Filipino, a direct command can feel rude — like ordering someone around. The prefix paki- (and the word naman) are your social oil. They turn demands into warm requests!
🚲 THE BICYCLE CHAIN RULE
No paki- → Squeaky, dry chain 😬 → "Bukas ng pinto!" (Open the door!) — sounds bossy!

With paki- → Smooth, oiled chain 😊 → "Pakibukas ng pinto." (Please open the door.) — sounds polite!
How does Paki- work?
You just add paki- to the beginning of an action word (verb):
paki- + ulit = Paki-ulit po
ulit = repeat → Paki-ulit = please repeat
paki- + abot = Pakiabot po
abot = pass/hand → Pakiabot = please pass
paki- + bukas = Pakibukas po
bukas = open → Pakibukas = please open
